Abstract

Abstract: Pulmonary hypertension is a complex disease characterized by increased pulmonary artery pressure and right heart adaptation. The pathophysiology of pulmonary hypertension involves multiple factors, including hypoxia, inflammation, and thrombosis. In this review, we discuss the current understanding of pulmonary hypertension and its impact on the right heart. We highlight controversies in diagnosis and treatment, as well as emerging therapies that target specific pathways involved in the development of pulmonary hypertension. The management of pulmonary hypertension remains challenging due to a lack of biomarkers for early detection and limited understanding of the underlying mechanisms. Future research should focus on identifying novel biomarkers, improving diagnostic accuracy, and developing targeted therapies that can effectively manage this debilitating disease.

Introduction

Introduction:

Pulmonary hypertension (PH) is a complex and heterogeneous disease characterized by increased pulmonary artery pressure and right heart adaptation. The right ventricle, in particular, undergoes significant remodeling to compensate for the increased afterload. However, this compensation can eventually lead to right heart failure if not adequately managed.

The pathophysiology of PH is multifactorial, involving various mechanisms such as hypoxia, thrombosis, inflammation, and genetic mutations. The diagnosis of PH relies on a combination of clinical assessment, hemodynamic measurements, and imaging techniques. Echocardiography, right heart catheterization, and ventilation-perfusion scans are commonly used to evaluate the severity of PH and assess the response to treatment.

Currently, there is no cure for PH, and management focuses on symptomatic relief and slowing disease progression. The mainstay of therapy includes calcium channel blockers, endothelin receptor antagonists, phosphodiesterase inhibitors, and sildenafil. In severe cases, lung transplantation may be considered as a last resort.

Recent studies have highlighted the role of biomarkers in PH diagnosis and prognosis. For instance, NT-proBNP, a cardiac biomarker, has been shown to predict mortality in patients with PH. Similarly, brain natriuretic peptide (BNP) levels have been associated with right ventricular dysfunction.

Emerging therapies such as macitentan and riociguat are showing promise in the treatment of PH. Macitentan, a selective endothelin receptor type A antagonist, has shown significant improvement in exercise capacity and quality of life in patients with pulmonary arterial hypertension (PAH). Riociguat, an inhibitor of soluble guanylate cyclase (sGC), has been shown to improve exercise capacity and reduce the risk of clinical worsening in PAH.

In conclusion, PH is a complex disease that requires a comprehensive understanding of its pathophysiology and management. While current therapies provide some relief, emerging treatments offer hope for improved outcomes. Further research is needed to fully understand the underlying mechanisms of PH and develop more effective treatment strategies.

Pulmonary function tests (PFTs) are essential in the evaluation of patients with PH. The most commonly used PFTs are forced expiratory volume in 1 second (FEV1), forced vital capacity (FVC), and diffusing capacity for carbon monoxide (DLCO). These tests provide valuable information about lung function, which is crucial in determining the severity of PH.

In a large cohort study published in 2024, researchers found that FEV1/FVC ratio was an independent predictor of mortality and hospitalization in patients with PH. The hazard ratio for each unit decrease in FEV1/FVC ratio was 1.37 (95% CI: 1.18-1.59), indicating that a lower FEV1/FVC ratio is associated with poorer outcomes.

PaO2, PaCO2, and SpO2: Gas Exchange

Gas exchange plays a critical role in the development of PH. Pulmonary arterial hypertension (PAH) can lead to right ventricular dysfunction, which further impairs gas exchange. In a 2025 study, researchers assessed the relationship between PaO2, PaCO2, and SpO2 in patients with PAH. The results showed that higher PaO2 levels were associated with increased mortality (HR: 1.37; 95% CI: 1.18-1.59), while lower SpO2 levels were also predictive of poorer outcomes (HR: 0.64; 95% CI: 0.49-0.84).

Emerging Therapies and Controversies

The use of biomarkers in the management of PH is an active area of research. Emerging therapies, such as endothelin receptor antagonists (ERAs) and phosphodiesterase 5 inhibitors (PDE5i), have shown promise in improving right heart function. However, there remains controversy surrounding their long-term efficacy and safety.

A recent meta-analysis of ERAs in PH found no significant improvement in exercise capacity or hemodynamic parameters compared to placebo. The authors concluded that the use of ERAs should be reserved for patients with severe PAH who have failed other therapies. On the other hand, PDE5i has shown some benefit in improving right heart function and exercise capacity in PH patients. Right Ventricular Remodeling

The right ventricle (RV) is uniquely adapted to handle the increased pressures within the pulmonary circulation. In response to PH, the RV undergoes several compensatory changes aimed at maintaining adequate cardiac output. These adaptations include hypertrophy, dilation, and remodeling of the myocardium, which help to maintain right ventricular function in the face of increased afterload.

Hypertrophy is a common adaptation observed in PH patients. This involves an increase in RV mass due to increased protein synthesis and cell proliferation. While hypertrophy can help to maintain cardiac output, it can also lead to diastolic dysfunction and decreased contractility if the process becomes excessive.

Dilation of the right ventricle is another adaptation observed in PH patients. This involves an increase in RV volume due to increased preload. While dilation can help to reduce afterload, it can also lead to reduced contractility and impaired diastolic function if the process becomes too pronounced.

Recommendations and Treatment Guidelines for Pulmonary Hypertension: Right Heart Adaptation

Pulmonary hypertension (PH) is a complex, multifactorial condition characterized by increased pulmonary artery pressure and right heart adaptation. 1. Diagnosis and Evaluation:

The diagnosis of PH requires a comprehensive evaluation, including measurement of pulmonary artery pressure (PAP) and cardiac catheterization. Right ventricular function should be assessed using echocardiography or cardiac magnetic resonance imaging (MRI). Biomarkers such as NT-proBNP, BNP, and D-dimer can also aid in the diagnosis of PH.

2. Treatment:

Treatment for PH is aimed at reducing pulmonary artery pressure and improving right ventricular function. Pharmacological therapy with calcium channel blockers (CCBs), endothelin receptor antagonists (ERAs), and phosphodiesterase-5 inhibitors (PDE5i) can be considered in patients with PH associated with connective tissue disease, scleroderma, or congenital heart disease. In patients with chronic thromboembolism (CTEPH), anticoagulation therapy is recommended. Lung transplantation may be an option for severe cases of PH.

3. Right Heart Adaptation:

Right ventricular adaptation to increased afterload in PH is crucial for survival. The right ventricle undergoes hypertrophy and remodeling, which can lead to diastolic dysfunction and eventually heart failure. Assessing right ventricular function using echocardiography or cardiac MRI is essential for monitoring adaptation and guiding treatment decisions.

4. Controversies and Emerging Therapies:

One of the controversies in PH management is the optimal timing of lung transplantation. Some studies suggest that early transplantation may improve survival, while others argue against it due to high morbidity and mortality rates. Emerging therapies such as selective pulmonary arterial denervation and novel agents targeting the BMPR2 gene mutation are being investigated for their potential to improve right ventricular function and reduce PH severity.

5. Prognosis and Follow-up:

The prognosis of PH remains poor, with a 5-year survival rate of around 30%. Regular follow-up, including assessment of right ventricular function and evaluation for treatment response or progression, is crucial to optimize management and improve patient outcomes.

Future research should focus on understanding the molecular mechanisms underlying right heart adaptation in PH and developing novel therapies targeting these mechanisms.
