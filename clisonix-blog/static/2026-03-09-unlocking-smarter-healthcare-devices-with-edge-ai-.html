The COVID-19 pandemic has accelerated the adoption of digital health technologies, putting pressure on healthcare providers to deliver high-quality care while minimizing costs. One critical area that requires attention is medical device development, where advances in AI and signal processing can unlock new possibilities for personalized medicine.

However, there's a pressing issue: current edge computing systems often fall short when it comes to handling the unique demands of EEG (electroencephalogram) signal processing. High-resolution data streams require significant computational resources, leading to latency issues that compromise patient care.

The Problem

Traditional approaches to edge computing rely on centralized architectures, where raw sensor data is transmitted to a cloud server for analysis and decision-making. This model has several drawbacks:

1. Latency: Delayed processing times can hinder real-time clinical decision-making. 2. Bandwidth: Transferring high-resolution EEG signals over networks consumes excessive bandwidth, causing connectivity issues. 3. Data Security: Sensitive medical data requires robust encryption and secure transmission protocols to prevent unauthorized access.

To overcome these limitations, healthcare device manufacturers must integrate AI capabilities directly into edge devices. This distributed computing approach enables real-time processing of sensor data, reducing latency and improving patient outcomes.
