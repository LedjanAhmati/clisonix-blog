In recent years, the field of brain-computer interfaces (BCIs) has made significant strides in developing technology that enables people to control devices with their thoughts. However, one of the major challenges facing BCIs is processing and analyzing the complex neural signals generated by electroencephalography (EEG). This is where wavelet decomposition comes into play.

The Problem

Current BCI systems rely heavily on traditional signal processing techniques, such as Fourier transforms, to analyze EEG signals. However, these methods have limitations when it comes to capturing the intricate patterns and features present in neural activity. Wavelet decomposition, on the other hand, offers a more robust and efficient way to process EEG signals by providing a time-frequency representation of the data.

Technical Deep Dive

Wavelet decomposition is a mathematical technique that breaks down a signal into its constituent frequency components using a set of basis functions called wavelets. This allows for the analysis of signals in both time and frequency domains simultaneously, making it an ideal choice for BCI applications where neural activity is highly dynamic.

At Clisonix, we've implemented wavelet decomposition as part of our LIAM Binary Algebra framework, which provides optimized, low-latency signal processing capabilities. Our implementation utilizes a combination of continuous and discrete wavelets to capture both the temporal and spectral characteristics of EEG signals.

fft = np.fft.rfft(raw_eeg, axis=1)
freqs = np.fft.rfftfreq(256, 1/256)
mask = (freqs >= 0.5) & (freqs <= 45)
filtered = np.fft.irfft(fft * mask, axis=1)

Feature extraction with matrix operations
