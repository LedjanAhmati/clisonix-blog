In recent years, electroencephalography (EEG) has emerged as a powerful tool for understanding the intricate workings of the human brain. With its ability to capture electrical signals from the scalp, EEG provides a unique window into brain function, allowing researchers to study cognitive processes, neurological disorders, and even brain-computer interfaces. However, processing and analyzing EEG data poses significant challenges, requiring sophisticated signal processing techniques and high-performance computing architectures.

The Problem: Real Challenges in EEG Neuroscience

EEG signals are inherently noisy and complex, with multiple frequency bands (e.g., alpha, beta, gamma) contributing to the overall activity. Current methods for extracting meaningful insights from EEG data often rely on manual feature engineering, which can be time-consuming and prone to human error. Furthermore, traditional computing approaches may not be able to handle the vast amounts of data generated by modern EEG systems, leading to lengthy processing times and reduced researcher productivity.
