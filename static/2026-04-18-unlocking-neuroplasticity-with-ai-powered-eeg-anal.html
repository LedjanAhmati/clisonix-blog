The Problem: Real Challenges in EEG Neuroscience

EEG (electroencephalography) has long been a cornerstone of neurological research and diagnosis, offering unparalleled insights into brain function. However, its full potential remains largely untapped due to the sheer volume of data generated by these recordings. Current analysis techniques often rely on manual processing, which is both time-consuming and prone to human error. Moreover, the complexity of EEG signals makes it challenging to extract meaningful information from these recordings.

Traditional approaches involve labor-intensive manual processing, where experts sift through countless hours of raw data to identify patterns and anomalies. This not only increases the risk of human bias but also limits the scope of analysis. In an era where neurological disorders are increasingly prevalent, it's clear that we need a more efficient and objective approach to EEG analysis.

To give you a taste of our technology in action, we've included an example code snippet that showcases the power of LIAM Binary Algebra:

Clisonix EEG Processing - Real Production Code

import numpy as np
from liam_core import BinaryAlgebra

EEG signal processing with vectorized operations

algebra = BinaryAlgebra()
raw_eeg = np.random.randn(64, 256)  # 64 channels, 256 samples

Bandpass filter via FFT (vectorized)

fft = np.fft.rfft(raw_eeg, axis=1)
freqs = np.fft.rfftfreq(256, 1/256)
mask = (freqs >= 0.5) & (freqs <= 45)
filtered = np.fft.irfft(fft * mask, axis=1)

Feature extraction with matrix operations

alpha_power = algebra.frobenius_norm(filtered[:, 8:13])
print(f"Alpha band power: {alpha_power:.2f}")
