In recent years, the healthcare industry has witnessed a surge in the development of brain-machine interfaces (BMIs). These innovative systems enable individuals to control devices or communicate through neural signals. However, one significant challenge in advancing BMIs is the interpretation and processing of EEG data. As researchers and clinicians push the boundaries of this technology, we need to address the complexities surrounding EEG neuroscience.

The Problem: Real Challenges in EEG Neuroscience

Current BMI systems rely heavily on EEG, which records electrical activity from the scalp. However, EEG signals are inherently noisy, making it challenging to accurately decode user intentions. Moreover, the sheer volume of data generated by these systems necessitates efficient processing and analysis techniques. To date, most EEG-based BMIs have struggled with:

1. Signal quality: Noisy or artifacts-contaminated EEG signals can lead to inaccurate decoding. 2. Data throughput: High-bandwidth EEG streams pose significant processing challenges. 3. Scalability: Current BMI systems often rely on expensive, specialized hardware.
