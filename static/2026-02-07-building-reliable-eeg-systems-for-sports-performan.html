The Problem

EEG-based systems are not without their limitations. One major issue is signal quality ‚Äì EEG signals are inherently noisy and prone to artifacts. Moreover, the sensitivity of EEG sensors requires careful calibration and placement to ensure accurate readings. Additionally, the sheer volume of data generated by EEG sensors can be overwhelming, requiring sophisticated algorithms for processing and analysis.

Another significant challenge lies in developing systems that can adapt to changing environments. In sports settings, athletes often move around or engage in high-intensity activities, which can disrupt EEG signals. Furthermore, EEG-based systems must be able to integrate with other data streams ‚Äì such as audio and biosensors ‚Äì to provide a comprehensive understanding of an athlete's performance.
