The Problem

EEG (electroencephalogram) signals are highly sensitive and contain valuable information about an individual's cognitive state and neurological function. However, when stored or shared without proper deidentification, these signals can be used to re-identify patients, compromising their anonymity and potentially exposing them to unauthorized access or exploitation. This is particularly concerning in the context of research studies, where participants' data may be aggregated and analyzed across multiple institutions.

According to a recent study, up to 75% of EEG data deidentification methods fail to protect patient identity adequately (1). Moreover, the use of inadequate deidentification techniques can lead to severe consequences, including reputational damage, regulatory penalties, and compromised trust in healthcare services.
