In recent years, the field of neuroscience has witnessed a surge in interest towards understanding the intricacies of brain function and its relationship to various neurological disorders. One crucial aspect of this endeavor is Electroencephalography (EEG) – a non-invasive technique used to record electrical activity from the scalp. While EEG holds immense promise for diagnosing and treating conditions such as epilepsy, Alzheimer's disease, and depression, traditional analysis methods often fall short in providing actionable insights.

The Problem: Real Challenges in EEG Neuroscience

Current approaches to EEG analysis rely heavily on manual processing, which can be time-consuming and prone to human error. Moreover, the sheer volume of data generated by modern EEG systems makes it challenging to extract meaningful information without sophisticated computational tools. As a result, researchers often struggle to:

1. Extract relevant features: Identifying patterns in EEG signals that correlate with specific neurological conditions is a daunting task. 2. Reduce dimensionality: The high-dimensional nature of EEG data poses significant challenges for analysis and interpretation. 3. Improve signal quality: Noisy or artifact-ridden signals can compromise the accuracy of downstream analyses.

Clisonix EEG Processing - Real Production Code

import numpy as np
from liam_core import BinaryAlgebra


EEG signal processing with vectorized operations

algebra = BinaryAlgebra()
raw_eeg = np.random.randn(64, 256)  # 64 channels, 256 samples

Bandpass filter via FFT (vectorized)

fft = np.fft.rfft(raw_eeg, axis=1)
freqs = np.fft.rfftfreq(256, 1/256)
mask = (freqs >= 0.5) & (freqs <= 45)
filtered = np.fft.irfft(fft * mask, axis=1)

Feature extraction with matrix operations

alpha_power = algebra.frobenius_norm(filtered[:, 8:13])
print(f"Alpha band power: {alpha_power:.2f}")
