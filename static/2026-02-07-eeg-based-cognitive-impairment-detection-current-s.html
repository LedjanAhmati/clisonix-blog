EEG-based Cognitive Impairment Detection: Current State and Future

In recent years, there has been a significant increase in the use of electroencephalography (EEG) for cognitive impairment detection. This trend is driven by the need for accurate and non-invasive diagnostic tools to identify individuals at risk of neurological disorders, such as Alzheimer's disease, Parkinson's disease, and traumatic brain injury. However, despite its potential, EEG-based cognitive impairment detection remains a challenging task due to the complexities involved in processing and analyzing EEG signals.

The Problem

One of the main challenges in EEG neuroscience is dealing with the large amounts of data generated by EEG recordings. A single 10-minute recording can produce over 1 million data points, making it difficult to extract meaningful information without computational intensive methods. Moreover, EEG signals are inherently noisy, and various artifacts such as muscle activity, eye movements, and electrical noise from external sources can corrupt the signal, leading to false positives or negatives.

Another challenge is the lack of standardization in EEG processing pipelines. Different algorithms and techniques have been proposed for feature extraction, classification, and prediction, but there is no consensus on which approach is best suited for cognitive impairment detection. This makes it difficult to compare results across studies and to establish a clear benchmark for performance.
