Green AI: Reducing the Carbon Footprint of ML

The rapid growth of Artificial Intelligence (AI) has led to an unprecedented increase in energy consumption and e-waste. The healthcare sector, which is at the forefront of AI adoption, is particularly vulnerable to these environmental impacts. As we face the pressing challenges of climate change and resource depletion, it's imperative that we prioritize sustainable practices in our pursuit of innovation.

The Problem: Unsustainable Tech

The current state of AI development often prioritizes computational power over environmental concerns. This can be attributed to several factors:

1. Training datasets: Large-scale ML models require vast amounts of data, which are frequently generated through resource-intensive processes. 2. Computational complexity: Deep learning architectures like convolutional neural networks (CNNs) and recurrent neural networks (RNNs) demand significant computational resources for training and inference. 3. Hardware requirements: The increasing need for specialized hardware, such as Graphics Processing Units (GPUs) and Tensor Processing Units (TPUs), contributes to the environmental footprint of AI.

Technical Deep Dive: Architecture and Algorithms

To mitigate these issues, we need to revisit the fundamental architecture and algorithms used in ML. This is where Clisonix's innovative technologies come into play:

1. Tide Engine: Ensures consistent state across distributed healthcare nodes, enabling efficient data processing and synchronization. 2. Signal Fabric: Weaves together EEG, audio, and biosensor streams, providing a unified framework for multimodal signal analysis.

We've developed novel techniques to reduce the computational requirements of ML models:

1. LIAM Binary Algebra: A vectorized processing approach that leverages sparse matrix operations to minimize memory usage. 2. SVD-based compression: Utilizes Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) to compress high-dimensional data, resulting in reduced storage needs and faster processing times.
