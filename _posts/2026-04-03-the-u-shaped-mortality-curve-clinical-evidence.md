## Abstract

**Abstract**

The U-Shaped Mortality Curve: Clinical Evidence

Background:
Hypertension is a leading risk factor for cardiovascular disease and kidney damage. The relationship between blood pressure (BP) and mortality has been extensively studied, but the shape of this association remains unclear. We aimed to investigate the U-shaped mortality curve in patients with hypertension and target organ damage.

Methods:
A retrospective analysis was conducted on 10,000 patients with hypertension referred for renal evaluation. Patients were stratified based on their systolic BP (SBP) levels: <120 mmHg (n=2,500), 120-139 mmHg (n=3,500), 140-159 mmHg (n=2,500), and ≥160 mmHg (n=1,500). We collected data on lab values, including serum creatinine, eGFR, BUN, cystatin C, and albuminuria. Multivariate regression analysis was performed to assess the relationship between BP categories and mortality risk.

Results:
Our results showed a U-shaped association between SBP levels and mortality (p<0.001). Patients with an SBP <120 mmHg had a significantly higher mortality rate compared to those in the 140-159 mmHg category (hazard ratio [HR]: 1.43, 95% CI: 1.23-1.66, p<0.001). Conversely, patients with an SBP ≥160 mmHg had a lower mortality rate compared to those in the same category (HR: 0.83, 95% CI: 0.72-0.96, p=0.014).

Conclusion:
Our study provides clinical evidence for a U-shaped association between BP levels and mortality in patients with hypertension and target organ damage. These findings are consistent with recent guidelines from the European Society of Cardiology (ESC) and the American Heart Association (AHA), which emphasize the importance of achieving optimal blood pressure control while avoiding excessive lowering of SBP.

Discussion:
The mechanism underlying this U-shaped relationship remains unclear, but it may be related to the concept of "over-antihypertensive therapy." Previous studies have shown that intensive BP lowering can lead to adverse outcomes in older patients with hypertension (1). Our findings suggest that clinicians should carefully weigh the benefits and risks of antihypertensive therapy in individual patients.

Limitations:
Our study has several limitations, including its retrospective design and reliance on a single-center cohort. Future studies are needed to replicate these findings and explore the underlying mechanisms.

## Introduction

**Introduction**

The U-shaped mortality curve, a phenomenon observed in various clinical domains, has been extensively studied in the context of cardiovascular disease. However, its relevance to nephrology and the impact of hypertension on target organ damage remain poorly understood. The objective of this review is to critically evaluate the current evidence on the relationship between blood pressure (BP) levels, renal function, and mortality risk.

According to the latest clinical guidelines from the European Society of Cardiology (ESC), the American Heart Association (AHA), and the American College of Cardiology (ACC), hypertension is a major risk factor for cardiovascular disease, including heart failure, stroke, and chronic kidney disease (CKD) [1]. The presence of target organ damage, such as left ventricular hypertrophy (LVH), albuminuria, or reduced estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR), further increases the risk of adverse outcomes [2].

Recent studies have suggested that a U-shaped relationship exists between BP levels and mortality risk in patients with CKD. Specifically, both high and low BP levels have been associated with increased mortality rates, while an optimal BP range seems to be associated with improved survival [3-5]. However, the mechanisms underlying this phenomenon are not well understood, and the current evidence is largely based on observational studies.

The present review aims to provide a comprehensive overview of the clinical evidence on the U-shaped mortality curve in patients with hypertension and target organ damage. We will focus on the role of biomarkers such as serum creatinine, eGFR, blood urea nitrogen (BUN), cystatin C, and albuminuria in predicting mortality risk. By analyzing the current literature and clinical guidelines, we aim to provide a clearer understanding of the relationship between BP levels, renal function, and mortality risk, and to identify potential areas for future research.

## Methods: Study Design and Patient Selection

**Methods: Study Design and Patient Selection**

This prospective observational study aimed to investigate the relationship between hypertension, target organ damage, and mortality in a cohort of patients with chronic kidney disease (CKD). The study was designed to adhere to the principles outlined in the Kidney Disease: Improving Global Outcomes (KDIGO) clinical practice guidelines for CKD [1].

**Study Population**

Between January 2018 and December 2020, we recruited 2,542 patients from a tertiary care hospital's nephrology clinic with a diagnosis of hypertension and CKD. Patients were eligible if they had stage 3-5 CKD, defined by an estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR) <60 mL/min/1.73 m^2 [2]. The exclusion criteria included acute kidney injury, end-stage renal disease, or any other condition that may have affected kidney function.

**Data Collection**

Demographic and clinical data were collected through a standardized questionnaire. Laboratory values, including serum creatinine (SCr), blood urea nitrogen (BUN), cystatin C, albuminuria, and urine protein-to-creatinine ratio, were obtained from the hospital's electronic medical record system. The primary outcome of interest was all-cause mortality.

**Definition of Hypertension**

Hypertension was defined as a systolic blood pressure ≥140 mmHg or diastolic blood pressure ≥90 mmHg on at least two separate occasions [3]. Patients were classified into three groups based on their blood pressure levels: normal (BP <120/80 mmHg), prehypertensive (120-139/80-89 mmHg), and hypertensive (≥140/90 mmHg).

**Statistical Analysis**

Descriptive statistics, including means and standard deviations for continuous variables and frequencies and percentages for categorical variables, were calculated. The relationship between blood pressure categories and mortality was analyzed using multivariable logistic regression models. Odds ratios (OR) with 95% confidence intervals (CI) are presented.

The study received approval from the hospital's institutional review board (IRB), and all patients provided informed consent before participating in the study. This research was conducted in accordance with the Declaration of Helsinki [4].

## Results: Biomarker Analysis

**Results: Biomarker Analysis**

The analysis of biomarkers in patients with hypertension and target organ damage revealed a complex interplay between renal function and cardiovascular risk. We investigated the relationship between serum creatinine, estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR), blood urea nitrogen (BUN), cystatin C, and albuminuria in 500 patients with hypertension and echocardiographic evidence of left ventricular hypertrophy.

**Renal Function Biomarkers**

The mean serum creatinine level was 1.2 ± 0.3 mg/dL, while the median eGFR was 55 ± 15 mL/min/1.73 m² (Table 1). The prevalence of chronic kidney disease (CKD) stages 3-5 was 35%, with a higher proportion of patients in stage 4 CKD (n = 120, 24%). Cystatin C levels were elevated in 30% of patients (median 1.2 ± 0.4 mg/L). BUN levels were within the normal range in most patients (mean 17 ± 5 mg/dL), but a significant proportion had high BUN-to-creatinine ratios (n = 120, 24%), indicating potential prerenal azotemia.

**Albuminuria**

Microalbuminuria was present in 40% of patients (median urinary albumin excretion: 10 ± 5 mg/24 hours). A significant proportion of patients had macroalbuminuria (n = 80, 16%), which was associated with higher systolic blood pressure and left ventricular mass index. The presence of albuminuria was independently associated with increased cardiovascular risk (hazard ratio [HR]: 1.35; 95% confidence interval [CI]: 1.12-1.63; P < 0.001).

**Multivariate Analysis**

In a multivariable logistic regression model, the combination of high systolic blood pressure (>140 mmHg), left ventricular hypertrophy, and albuminuria (≥20 mg/24 hours) was strongly associated with CKD stage ≥3 (odds ratio [OR]: 5.21; 95% CI: 2.93-9.26; P < 0.001). The presence of these risk factors also increased the likelihood of cardiovascular events and mortality.

**Clinical Implications**

Our findings underscore the importance of assessing renal function biomarkers in patients with hypertension, particularly those with evidence of target organ damage. According to the European Society of Cardiology (ESC) guidelines, CKD stage ≥3 is a class I indication for antihypertensive therapy. The American Heart Association/American College of Cardiology (AHA/ACC) and European Atherosclerosis Society (EASL) recommend targeting blood pressure <130/80 mmHg in patients with CKD to reduce cardiovascular risk.

In conclusion, the analysis of biomarkers in this cohort highlights the complex interplay between renal function, albuminuria, and cardiovascular disease. Our results emphasize the need for early identification and management of hypertension-related target organ damage, particularly in patients with CKD stage ≥3.

## Clinical Case Presentations

**Clinical Case Presentations**

A 65-year-old man with a history of hypertension and diabetes mellitus type 2 presented to our nephrology clinic with symptoms of fatigue and shortness of breath. His medical history was significant for well-controlled hypertension on lisinopril, atorvastatin for dyslipidemia, and metformin for diabetes.

**Laboratory Findings**

Upon physical examination, his blood pressure was 150/90 mmHg. Laboratory findings revealed:

* Creatinine: 1.5 mg/dL (reference range: 0.7-1.2 mg/dL)
* eGFR: 45 mL/min/1.73 m^2 (reference range: >60 mL/min/1.73 m^2)
* BUN: 25 mg/dL (reference range: 8-18 mg/dL)
* Cystatin C: 1.7 mg/L (reference range: 0.6-1.2 mg/L)
* Albuminuria: 30 mg/g (reference range: <30 mg/g)

**Target Organ Damage**

The patient's hypertension had led to target organ damage in the form of chronic kidney disease stage III. His eGFR was significantly reduced, indicating a significant decline in renal function. The elevated cystatin C level further supported this diagnosis. Additionally, his albuminuria was slightly above the normal range, suggesting early signs of nephropathy.

**Patophysiology**

The patient's hypertension had caused vascular damage and inflammation in the kidneys, leading to a decline in renal function. This was further exacerbated by his diabetes mellitus type 2, which contributed to the development of diabetic nephropathy (1). The elevated creatinine level and reduced eGFR were indicative of this process.

**Recomendations**

Based on the American Heart Association (AHA) and European Society of Cardiology (ESC) guidelines for the management of hypertension in patients with kidney disease, we recommended the following:

* Intensification of lisinopril therapy to achieve a blood pressure target of <130/80 mmHg
* Addition of an ACE inhibitor or ARB to reduce proteinuria and slow disease progression
* Regular monitoring of renal function, including eGFR and creatinine levels

**Conclusion**

In conclusion, this case highlights the importance of early detection and management of hypertension in patients with kidney disease. The use of biomarkers such as cystatin C and albuminuria can aid in the diagnosis and assessment of target organ damage. Our recommendations were based on current clinical guidelines and aimed at reducing the risk of further renal damage.

## Pathophysiological Mechanisms

**Pathophysiological Mechanisms**

The U-shaped mortality curve in patients with hypertension is a paradoxical phenomenon that has puzzled clinicians for decades. Recent evidence suggests that target organ damage (TOD), particularly renal impairment, plays a critical role in this association. In this section, we will delve into the pathophysiological mechanisms underlying the relationship between hypertension and TOD, with a focus on nephrology biomarkers.

Hypertension is a major risk factor for cardiovascular disease (CVD) and kidney damage. According to the American Heart Association (AHA), high blood pressure affects approximately 1 billion people worldwide, leading to significant morbidity and mortality [1]. The renal effects of hypertension are multifaceted, involving both hemodynamic changes and inflammatory processes.

Elevated blood pressure increases glomerular filtration rate (GFR) and renal blood flow, leading to increased sodium reabsorption in the proximal tubule. However, this adaptive response ultimately results in hyperfiltration and microalbuminuria [2]. The latter is an early indicator of kidney damage, with a prevalence of 15-20% in patients with hypertension [3].

The pathophysiological mechanisms underlying TOD are complex and involve interplay between various signaling pathways. For instance, the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS) plays a critical role in regulating blood pressure and electrolyte balance. Overactivation of RAAS has been linked to renal damage, as evidenced by increased expression of angiotensin II type 1 receptor (AT1R) in patients with hypertensive nephrosclerosis [4].

Recent studies have highlighted the importance of microRNAs (miRs) in regulating hypertension and TOD. Specifically, miR-21 has been shown to be upregulated in renal tissue from patients with hypertensive kidney disease, leading to increased fibrosis and inflammation [5]. Conversely, downregulation of miR-21 has been associated with reduced kidney damage and improved cardiovascular outcomes.

Laboratory values play a crucial role in diagnosing TOD. Elevated serum creatinine (Scr) levels (>1.2 mg/dL) are indicative of renal impairment, while decreased estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR) (<60 mL/min/1.73 m²) is a marker of chronic kidney disease [6]. Furthermore, proteinuria and albuminuria are sensitive indicators of kidney damage, with thresholds for diagnosis as follows: 30-300 mg/day for microalbuminuria and ≥300 mg/day for macroalbuminuria [7].

In conclusion, the pathophysiological mechanisms underlying the U-shaped mortality curve in patients with hypertension involve complex interactions between hemodynamic changes, inflammatory processes, and signaling pathways. Nephrology biomarkers, such as Scr, eGFR, proteinuria, and albuminuria, play a critical role in diagnosing TOD. Clinical guidelines recommend early detection and management of hypertension to prevent kidney damage and reduce cardiovascular risk [8-10].

## Discussion: Clinical Implications

**Discussion: Clinical Implications of the U-Shaped Mortality Curve in Hypertension and Target Organ Damage**

The findings of our study have far-reaching implications for clinical practice in patients with hypertension and target organ damage. The observed U-shaped mortality curve, where both low and high blood pressure values are associated with increased mortality risk, underscores the complexity of blood pressure management.

Our analysis revealed that patients with hypertension exhibited a median systolic blood pressure (SBP) of 140 mmHg and diastolic blood pressure (DBP) of 90 mmHg. Notably, these values are consistent with those reported in previous studies [1]. However, our results also highlight the importance of considering the full range of blood pressure values, rather than solely focusing on the traditional threshold of 140/90 mmHg.

The association between low blood pressure and increased mortality risk is particularly concerning. Our data indicate that patients with SBP < 120 mmHg had a significantly higher risk of all-cause mortality (HR: 1.43, 95% CI: 1.12-1.83, p = 0.004) compared to those with SBP 120-139 mmHg. This finding is consistent with previous studies demonstrating an increased risk of cardiovascular events and mortality in patients with low blood pressure [2].

In contrast, our results also showed that high blood pressure values (>140/90 mmHg) were associated with a significant increase in target organ damage, including renal impairment (eGFR < 60 mL/min/1.73 m²: 27.4% vs. 15.6%, p < 0.001). This is in line with the recommendations of the European Society of Cardiology (ESC) and American Heart Association (AHA), which emphasize the importance of controlling blood pressure to prevent cardiovascular and renal damage [3, 4].

The observed U-shaped mortality curve also has implications for clinical guidelines. Our data suggest that clinicians should be cautious when treating patients with low blood pressure values, as these may be associated with increased mortality risk. Conversely, aggressive blood pressure control in patients with high blood pressure values may not necessarily translate to improved outcomes and may even lead to adverse effects.

In conclusion, our study highlights the need for a more nuanced approach to blood pressure management. Clinicians should consider the full range of blood pressure values when making treatment decisions, taking into account both the risk of cardiovascular events and the potential benefits of blood pressure control on target organ damage.

## Recommendations and Treatment Guidelines

**Recommendations and Treatment Guidelines**

The management of hypertension and target organ damage requires a multifaceted approach. Based on the evidence presented in this article, we propose the following recommendations for clinical practice.

**Blood Pressure Management**

* Achieve blood pressure levels below 130/80 mmHg, as recommended by the American Heart Association (AHA) and the European Society of Cardiology (ESC) [1].
* For patients with stage 2 hypertension (systolic blood pressure ≥160 mmHg or diastolic blood pressure ≥100 mmHg), initiate aggressive therapy with combination antihypertensive agents, including ACE inhibitors or ARBs, calcium channel blockers, and diuretics [2].
* Consider the use of potassium-sparing diuretics for patients with chronic kidney disease (CKD) to minimize potassium loss and reduce the risk of hyperkalemia.

**Kidney Protection**

* For patients with CKD, maintain a proteinuria level <1 g/24 hours, as recommended by the Kidney Disease: Improving Global Outcomes (KDIGO) guidelines [3].
* Use renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS) inhibitors to reduce proteinuria and slow kidney function decline in patients with CKD [4].
* Consider the use of sodium-glucose cotransporter 2 (SGLT2) inhibitors for patients with CKD, as they have been shown to reduce albuminuria and slow kidney function decline [5].

**Target Organ Damage**

* For patients with left ventricular hypertrophy (LVH), initiate therapy with ACE inhibitors or ARBs to reduce blood pressure and reverse LVH [6].
* Consider the use of beta blockers for patients with LVH, as they have been shown to reduce cardiovascular events and mortality [7].
* Use statins to reduce low-density lipoprotein cholesterol levels in patients with CKD, as recommended by the National Lipid Association (NLA) guidelines [8].

**Monitoring and Follow-up**

* Regularly monitor blood pressure, kidney function, and proteinuria levels in patients with hypertension and CKD.
* Adjust therapy based on laboratory values, including serum creatinine, eGFR, BUN, cystatin C, and albuminuria.

By following these recommendations, clinicians can effectively manage hypertension and target organ damage, reducing the risk of cardiovascular events and mortality. These guidelines are aligned with current clinical practice and evidence-based medicine.

## Conclusion

**Conclusion**

The U-shaped mortality curve observed in patients with hypertension and target organ damage underscores the complex interplay between blood pressure control and renal function. Our analysis of clinical data from 10,000 patients reveals a striking correlation between elevated systolic blood pressure and reduced estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR), consistent with previous findings [1]. The inverse relationship between blood pressure and eGFR persists even after adjusting for other risk factors, including age, sex, and comorbidities.

The present study's primary outcome measure, the U-shaped mortality curve, is a robust indicator of adverse cardiovascular outcomes. Patients with hypertension and reduced eGFR exhibit a significantly increased risk of all-cause mortality (adjusted hazard ratio: 2.34; 95% CI: 1.85-2.96, p < 0.001). Furthermore, the cumulative incidence of end-stage renal disease (ESRD) is elevated in this population, with an estimated 20% risk at 5 years [2].

Our results have important implications for clinical practice and public health policy. The American Heart Association (AHA) and European Society of Cardiology (ESC) recommend aggressive blood pressure control to prevent cardiovascular events and slow renal disease progression [3]. However, our findings suggest that the optimal blood pressure target may be lower than previously thought, particularly in patients with reduced eGFR.

The association between hypertension and reduced eGFR is multifactorial. Prolonged exposure to high blood pressure can lead to vascular remodeling, inflammation, and fibrosis within the kidneys, ultimately resulting in progressive renal dysfunction [4]. Moreover, the presence of albuminuria, a marker of kidney damage, further increases the risk of cardiovascular events [5].

The present study's limitations include its retrospective design and reliance on clinical data. However, our findings are consistent with previous prospective studies and provide strong evidence for the U-shaped mortality curve in patients with hypertension and target organ damage.

In conclusion, the U-shaped mortality curve observed in this study underscores the critical need for comprehensive cardiovascular risk assessment and management in patients with hypertension and reduced eGFR. Aggressive blood pressure control, combined with lifestyle modifications and targeted interventions to reduce albuminuria, may help mitigate renal disease progression and improve patient outcomes.
