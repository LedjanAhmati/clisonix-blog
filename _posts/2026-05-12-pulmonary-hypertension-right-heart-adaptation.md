## Abstract

 Abstract: Pulmonary hypertension is a complex disease characterized by increased pulmonary artery pressure and right heart adaptation. The pathophysiology of pulmonary hypertension involves multiple factors, including hypoxia, inflammation, and thrombosis. In this review, we discuss the current understanding of pulmonary hypertension and its impact on the right heart. We highlight controversies in diagnosis and treatment, as well as emerging therapies that target specific pathways involved in the development of pulmonary hypertension. The management of pulmonary hypertension remains challenging due to a lack of biomarkers for early detection and limited understanding of the underlying mechanisms. ## Introduction

 Introduction:
Pulmonary hypertension (PH) is a complex and heterogeneous disease characterized by increased pulmonary artery pressure and right heart adaptation. The right ventricle, in particular, undergoes significant remodeling to compensate for the increased afterload. However, this compensation can eventually lead to right heart failure if not adequately managed.

The pathophysiology of PH is multifactorial, involving various mechanisms such as hypoxia, thrombosis, inflammation, and genetic mutations. The diagnosis of PH relies on a combination of clinical assessment, hemodynamic measurements, and imaging techniques. Echocardiography, right heart catheterization, and ventilation-perfusion scans are commonly used to evaluate the severity of PH and assess the response to treatment.

Currently, there is no cure for PH, and management focuses on symptomatic relief and slowing disease progression. The mainstay of therapy includes calcium channel blockers, endothelin receptor antagonists, phosphodiesterase inhibitors, and sildenafil. In severe cases, lung transplantation may be considered as a last resort.

Recent studies have highlighted the role of biomarkers in PH diagnosis and prognosis. For instance, NT-proBNP, a cardiac biomarker, has been shown to predict mortality in patients with PH. Similarly, brain natriuretic peptide (BNP) levels have been associated with right ventricular dysfunction.

Emerging therapies such as macitentan and riociguat are showing promise in the treatment of PH. Macitentan, a selective endothelin receptor type A antagonist, has shown significant improvement in exercise capacity and quality of life in patients with pulmonary arterial hypertension (PAH). Riociguat, an inhibitor of soluble guanylate cyclase (sGC), has been shown to improve exercise capacity and reduce the risk of clinical worsening in PAH.

In conclusion, PH is a complex disease that requires a comprehensive understanding of its pathophysiology and management. While current therapies provide some relief, emerging treatments offer hope for improved outcomes. Further research is needed to fully understand the underlying mechanisms of PH and develop more effective treatment strategies.
