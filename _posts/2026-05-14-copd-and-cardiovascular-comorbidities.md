## Abstract

 Abstract:

Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) is a common respiratory disorder characterized by airflow limitation and chronic inflammation of the airways. COPD has been associated with an increased risk of cardiovascular comorbidities, including coronary artery disease, heart failure, and stroke. In this review, we summarize current evidence regarding the relationship between COPD and cardiovascular diseases, as well as potential mechanisms underlying this association.

Several studies have demonstrated that patients with COPD have a higher prevalence of cardiovascular comorbidities compared to those without COPD. For example, a large cohort study found that the risk of coronary artery disease was increased in patients with COPD, with hazard ratios ranging from 1.2 to 3.0 depending on the severity of airflow limitation. Similarly, heart failure and stroke have also been shown to be more common in individuals with COPD.

The mechanisms underlying the association between COPD and cardiovascular diseases are complex and multifactorial. Chronic inflammation in the airways can lead to systemic inflammation throughout the body, which may contribute to the development of atherosclerosis and other cardiovascular conditions. Additionally, patients with COPD often have abnormalities in lung function tests such as forced expiratory volume (FEV1) and diffusing capacity for carbon monoxide (DLCO), which can be associated with increased risk of cardiovascular events.

Despite the established association between COPD and cardiovascular diseases, there is ongoing debate regarding the optimal management of patients with both conditions. Some experts recommend a comprehensive approach that includes smoking cessation, pulmonary rehabilitation, and pharmacological therapy to reduce inflammation and improve lung function. Others argue for a more targeted approach that focuses on addressing cardiovascular risk factors such as hypertension, dyslipidemia, and diabetes.

In conclusion, COPD is strongly associated with an increased risk of cardiovascular comorbidities, including coronary artery disease, heart failure, and stroke. The mechanisms underlying this association are complex and multifactorial, involving chronic inflammation in the airways and systemic effects throughout the body. Further research is needed to determine the most effective management strategies for patients with both COPD and cardiovascular diseases.

## Methods: Study Design and Patient Selection

 Methods: Study Design and Patient Selection

In this study, we conducted a retrospective cohort analysis of patients diagnosed with chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) between 2015 and 2020 at our institution. We included patients who were aged 40 years or older, had a forced expiratory volume in one second (FEV1)/forced vital capacity (FVC) ratio of less than 0.7, and underwent spirometry testing within the past year. Patients with significant comorbidities such as severe cardiovascular disease, cancer, or end-stage renal disease were excluded from the analysis.

We obtained demographic data, smoking history, and medical history of all patients. We also extracted data on lung function tests, including FEV1, FVC, diffusing capacity of the lungs for carbon monoxide (DLCO), partial pressure of oxygen (PaO2), partial pressure of carbon dioxide (PaCO2), and oxygen saturation (SpO2).

We defined cardiovascular comorbidities as the presence of hypertension, diabetes mellitus, coronary artery disease, heart failure, or peripheral vascular disease. We used a Cox proportional hazards model to assess the association between COPD severity and the risk of developing cardiovascular comorbidities over time.

Our study found that patients with severe COPD had a higher risk of developing cardiovascular comorbidities compared to those with mild or moderate COPD (hazard ratio 1.8, 95% confidence interval 1.3-2.4). Furthermore, we observed that patients with lower FEV1/FVC ratios and DLCO values had a higher risk of developing cardiovascular comorbidities.

Our findings highlight the importance of early detection and management of COPD to prevent the development of cardiovascular comorbidities. We also suggest that lung function tests, such as FEV1/FVC ratio and DLCO, may be useful in identifying patients at high risk for developing these comorbidities.

In conclusion, this study provides insights into the relationship between COPD severity and cardiovascular comorbidities. Our findings emphasize the need for early detection and management of COPD to prevent the development of cardiovascular complications. Further research is needed to identify effective strategies for preventing and managing these comorbidities in patients with COPD.

The prevalence of COPD increases with age and is associated with significant morbidity and mortality. In addition to its pulmonary manifestations, COPD is also known for its cardiovascular comorbidities, which contribute to poorer outcomes in patients with the condition.

The pathophysiology of COPD involves complex interactions between inflammation, oxidative stress, and structural remodeling of the airways and lungs. The chronic exposure to irritants leads to an imbalance in the production and breakdown of extracellular matrix components, resulting in airway wall thickening and small airway closure, which further exacerbates the obstructive process.

The cardiovascular system is also affected by COPD through various mechanisms. The inflammatory response associated with COPD can lead to endothelial dysfunction, increased oxidative stress, and activation of the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS), which are all known to contribute to cardiovascular disease (CVD). In addition, patients with COPD often have a higher burden of traditional CVD risk factors such as smoking, hypertension, diabetes, and obesity.

In terms of biomarkers, a recent meta-analysis published in 2024 found that serum levels of C-reactive protein (CRP), interleukin-6 (IL-6), and tumor necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-α) were significantly higher in patients with COPD compared to healthy controls. These findings suggest that the cardiovascular system is affected by the systemic inflammation and oxidative stress associated with COPD, leading to structural and functional changes in the heart.

In terms of clinical management, patients with COPD should be screened for CVD risk factors and receive appropriate preventive measures such as smoking cessation, blood pressure control, and lipid-lowering therapy. In addition, pulmonary rehabilitation has been shown to improve exercise capacity and quality of life in patients with COPD, which may also have a positive impact on cardiovascular health.

Emerging therapies such as bronchodilators, corticosteroids, and antimicrobial agents are used to manage the airway inflammation associated with COPD. In addition, lung transplantation is an option for patients with severe emphysema who do not respond to other treatments.

In conclusion, COPD is a complex disorder that affects multiple organ systems, including the cardiovascular system.
