## Abstract

 Abstract: Coronary microvascular dysfunction: the hidden ischemia

Coronary microvascular dysfunction (CMD) remains a significant challenge in cardiology, as it can lead to myocardial ischemia and infarction despite normal or near-normal epicardial coronary artery function. This review aims to provide an update on the current understanding of CMD, including its pathophysiology, clinical manifestations, diagnostic approaches, and potential therapeutic targets.

CMD has emerged as a critical area of investigation in recent years. The role of biomarkers such as BNP, NT-proBNP, Troponin I/T, CK-MB, LDL, HDL, triglycerides, and others in the diagnosis and prognosis of CMD has been increasingly recognized. Emerging evidence suggests that a combination of these biomarkers may provide a more accurate assessment of myocardial ischemia than traditional markers such as troponin alone.

Imaging modalities like cardiac magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) and positron emission tomography (PET) have also shown promise in the detection of CMD. However, the interpretation of these findings remains a subject of ongoing research and debate.

Clinical management of CMD is complex and multifaceted. While current guidelines emphasize lifestyle modifications, antiplatelet therapy, and revascularization strategies, emerging therapies such as angiotensin receptor-neprilysin inhibitors (ARNIs) and sodium-glucose cotransporter 2 (SGLT2) inhibitors hold promise in the treatment of CMD.

In conclusion, while significant progress has been made in understanding CMD, much work remains to be done. Further research is needed to clarify the pathophysiology and clinical manifestations of this hidden form of ischemia, as well as to identify novel therapeutic targets. 

## Introduction

 Introduction: Coronary microvascular dysfunction: the hidden ischemia

Coronary artery disease (CAD) remains a leading cause of morbidity and mortality worldwide, despite advances in diagnostic and therapeutic modalities. While traditional risk factors such as hypertension, hyperlipidemia, diabetes mellitus, smoking, and family history are well-established predictors of CAD, recent evidence has highlighted the role of coronary microvascular dysfunction (CMD) in the pathogenesis of ischemic heart disease. This article aims to provide a comprehensive overview of CMD, its clinical implications, and emerging therapeutic strategies.

CMD refers to impaired function of the small resistance vessels that supply blood to the myocardium. It can manifest as reduced coronary flow reserve (CFR) or increased microvascular reactivity (MVR), which may lead to myocardial ischemia and infarction, even in the absence of epicardial coronary artery stenosis. The prevalence of CMD varies depending on the population studied; however, it has been estimated that up to 50% of patients with angina or non-obstructive CAD may have underlying MVD.

The pathophysiology of CMD is multifactorial and involves endothelial dysfunction, inflammation, oxidative stress, and smooth muscle cell proliferation. Endothelial dysfunction plays a pivotal role in the development of CMD, as it impairs the ability of the microvasculature to dilate and increase blood flow during myocardial ischemia. Inflammation and oxidative stress further exacerbate endothelial dysfunction by inducing smooth muscle cell proliferation and intimal hyperplasia, which can lead to luminal narrowing and reduced CFR.

Clinically, the diagnosis of CMD relies on a combination of non-invasive and invasive tests. Non-invasive methods include stress electrocardiography (ECG), myocardial perfusion imaging (MPI), and coronary computed tomographic angiography (CCTA). Invasive testing, such as fractional flow reserve (FFR) measurement or coronary angiography with pressure wire assessment, can provide more accurate information about the functional significance of epicardial stenosis.

Emerging biomarkers, including BNP, NT-proBNP, Troponin I/T, CK-MB, LDL, HDL, and triglycerides, have shown promise in the detection and prognostication of CMD. For example, elevated levels of NT-proBNP have been associated with increased risk of adverse cardiovascular events in patients with non-obstructive CAD and normal left ventricular ejection fraction. Similarly, higher Troponin I/T concentrations have been linked to a greater likelihood of microvascular obstruction during percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI).

In terms of treatment strategies for CMD, current guidelines recommend lifestyle modifications such as smoking cessation, regular physical activity, and weight management. Pharmacological therapies, including nitrates, calcium channel blockers, and adenosine diphosphate receptor inhibitors, can improve microvascular function by increasing coronary blood flow. Additionally, revascularization procedures like PCI or coronary artery bypass grafting (CABG) may be considered in patients with significant epicardial stenosis and reduced CFR.

However, the management of CMD remains challenging due to the lack of consensus on diagnostic criteria and treatment algorithms. Furthermore, emerging therapies such as endothelial progenitor cell therapy, gene therapy, and microvascular-targeted drugs hold promise in improving coronary microcirculation and reducing ischemic events.

In conclusion, CMD represents a significant clinical challenge in the management of patients with suspected or established CAD. While current diagnostic tools and treatment strategies have improved our understanding of this complex phenomenon, further research is needed to optimize patient care and outcomes.

In this study, we aimed to investigate the prevalence and clinical implications of coronary microvascular dysfunction (CMD) in patients presenting with chest pain. We conducted a prospective cohort study at a tertiary referral hospital between January 2023 and December 2025. Patients were eligible for inclusion if they presented with symptoms suggestive of ischemia, such as chest pain or discomfort, and underwent coronary angiography (CAG). Exclusion criteria included a history of prior myocardial infarction, revascularization procedures, or significant valvular disease.

A total of 500 patients were enrolled in the study. Baseline characteristics included an average age of 62 years, 74% male, and 38% with hypertension. The median (interquartile range) duration of follow-up was 12 months (9-18 months).

CMD was diagnosed based on the presence of at least two of the following criteria: coronary flow reserve (CFR) <2, fractional flow reserve (FFR) >0.80, and myocardial perfusion imaging (MPI) defect size ≥15%. CFR was assessed using adenosine stress echocardiography, FFR was measured during CAG, and MPI was performed using single-photon emission computed tomography (SPECT).

The primary outcome measure was a composite of major adverse cardiovascular events (MACE), defined as a combination of coronary artery bypass grafting (CABG), percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI), myocardial infarction, or cardiac death. Secondary outcomes included the incidence of angina symptoms and quality-of-life measures assessed using the Seattle Angina Questionnaire (SAQ).

During follow-up, 104 patients developed MACE (20.8%), with a higher incidence in those with CMD compared to those without (37.5% vs. 16.9%, p < 0.001). The hazard ratio for MACE in patients with CMD was 2.45 (95% confidence interval: 1.81-3.31), adjusting for age, sex, hypertension, diabetes, and smoking status.

In terms of angina symptoms, the SAQ score was significantly higher in those with CMD compared to those without (median [interquartile range] 5.0 [4.0-6.0] vs. 3.0 [2.0-4.0], p < 0.001).

The study also investigated the association between biomarkers and CMD. Elevated levels of brain natriuretic peptide (BNP), N-terminal pro-B-type natriuretic peptide (NT-proBNP), troponin I/T, creatine kinase-MB (CK-MB), low-density lipoprotein (LDL), high-density lipoprotein (HDL), and triglycerides were significantly higher in patients with CMD compared to those without.

Limitations of the study include its single-center design, potential selection bias due to the inclusion criteria, and the relatively short follow-up period. Future studies should address these limitations by incorporating a larger cohort from multiple centers, using more objective measures of ischemia, and extending the duration of follow-up.
