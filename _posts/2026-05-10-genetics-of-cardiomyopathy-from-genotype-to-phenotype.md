 Introduction: Genetics of cardiomyopathy: from genotype to phenotype

Cardiomyopathy is a complex and heterogeneous group of disorders characterized by impaired ventricular function, which can be caused by various genetic and environmental factors. The identification of specific genes associated with cardiomyopathy has led to a better understanding of the underlying mechanisms and potential therapeutic targets. In this review, we will discuss the current state of knowledge regarding the genetics of cardiomyopathy, focusing on recent advances in molecular biology and genomics, as well as their clinical implications.

The genetic basis of cardiomyopathy is multifactorial, with both monogenic and polygenic inheritance patterns contributing to disease susceptibility. Monogenic forms are typically caused by mutations in a single gene, such as TTN, LMNA, or MYH7, which can result in specific subtypes of cardiomyopathy, including dilated, hypertrophic, and restrictive variants. Polygenic inheritance involves the interaction of multiple genes with various environmental factors, leading to a more gradual onset and diverse clinical phenotypes.

Recent genome-wide association studies (GWAS) have identified several novel loci associated with cardiomyopathy, including chromosomes 1p36, 2q35, and 4q25. These findings highlight the importance of understanding the genetic architecture of cardiomyopathy to develop targeted therapies and improve patient outcomes.

Biomarkers play a crucial role in the diagnosis and management of cardiomyopathy. Brain natriuretic peptide (BNP), N-terminal pro-brain natriuretic peptide (NT-proBNP), troponin I/T, creatine kinase-MB, LDL cholesterol, HDL cholesterol, and triglycerides are commonly used markers to assess cardiac injury and evaluate disease severity. Emerging biomarkers such as microRNAs (miRs) and circulating RNA fragments have shown promise in predicting adverse outcomes and identifying subtypes of cardiomyopathy.

Emerging therapies, including gene therapy, stem cell-based approaches, and targeted drug development, hold great potential for the treatment of cardiomyopathy. For example, the CARDIO-TTRansform trial investigates the efficacy of eplonersen in transthyretin amyloidosis cardiomyopathy, while other studies explore the role of angiotensin receptor neprilysin inhibitors and sodium-glucose cotransporter 2 (SGLT2) inhibitors.

Despite significant progress in understanding the genetics of cardiomyopathy, several challenges remain. The complexity of genetic interactions, the need for large-scale clinical trials, and the translation of molecular discoveries into effective therapies are some of the key areas that require further investigation.

In conclusion, the genetics of cardiomyopathy is a rapidly evolving field with significant implications for patient care. By continuing to unravel the complex interplay between genes and environment, we can develop more targeted and personalized treatments for this debilitating disorder.

## Pathophysiological Mechanisms

 Pathophysiological Mechanisms of Cardiomyopathy: From Genotype to Phenotype

Cardiomyopathy is a complex and heterogeneous disease, characterized by structural and functional abnormalities of the heart that can result from various genetic and environmental factors. The genetics of cardiomyopathy have become an increasingly important area of research in recent years, with advances in molecular biology and genomics providing new insights into the underlying mechanisms and potential therapeutic targets.

From a clinical perspective, cardiomyopathy can be broadly classified into three main types: dilated, hypertrophic, and restrictive. Each type is associated with specific genetic mutations and molecular pathways that contribute to the development of the disease. For example, dilated cardiomyopathy has been linked to mutations in genes such as TTN, LMNA, and MYH7, which can disrupt the normal functioning of the heart muscle and lead to impaired contractility and diastolic dysfunction.

In addition to genetic factors, environmental triggers such as viral infections or exposure to toxic substances can also play a role in the development of cardiomyopathy. Moreover, comorbidities such as hypertension, diabetes, and obesity are common risk factors for the disease, highlighting the importance of preventive measures and lifestyle modifications in reducing its incidence.

From a biomarker perspective, several molecules have been identified that can help diagnose and monitor cardiomyopathy. BNP (brain natriuretic peptide) and NT-proBNP (N-terminal pro brain natriuretic peptide) are two well-established biomarkers that reflect the degree of ventricular stretch and myocardial injury, respectively. Troponin I/T and CK-MB (creatine kinase muscle-brain fraction) are other biomarkers that can indicate myocardial damage or necrosis in acute cases of cardiomyopathy.

In terms of treatment options, current guidelines recommend a combination of pharmacological therapy, device-based interventions, and lifestyle modifications for patients with cardiomyopathy. Angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE) inhibitors or angiotensin receptor blockers (ARBs), beta-blockers, and mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists (MRAs) are commonly used drugs that can help reduce the workload on the heart muscle and improve cardiac function. Implantable cardioverter defibrillators (ICDs) and cardiac resynchronization therapy (CRT) devices can also be useful in preventing sudden death or improving survival rates in patients with advanced cardiomyopathy.

However, despite these advances, many challenges remain in the management of cardiomyopathy. One major limitation is the lack of effective therapies for some subtypes of the disease, such as those caused by genetic mutations that are not amenable to current drug treatments. Moreover, the high burden of comorbidities and social determinants of health in many patients with cardiomyopathy can make it difficult to implement evidence-based interventions and monitor their effectiveness over time.

In conclusion, the genetics of cardiomyopathy continue to be an active area of research, with new discoveries and therapeutic approaches emerging regularly. While significant progress has been made in understanding the molecular mechanisms underlying the disease, much work remains to be done in translating this knowledge into effective treatments that can improve outcomes for patients with cardiomyopathy.
