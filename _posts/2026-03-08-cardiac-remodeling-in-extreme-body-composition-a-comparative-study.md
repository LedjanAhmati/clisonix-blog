## Abstract

**Abstract**

Cardiac remodeling is a complex and multifactorial process that underlies the development of cardiovascular diseases (CVD) in extreme body compositions. In patients with morbid obesity or extreme lean mass depletion, cardiac hypertrophy and fibrosis are common features, often accompanied by left ventricular dilatation and decreased ejection fraction (LVEF). The molecular mechanisms driving these changes remain poorly understood.

Recent studies have highlighted the role of various biomarkers in predicting cardiovascular risk, including brain natriuretic peptide (BNP), N-terminal pro-b-type natriuretic peptide (NT-proBNP), troponin I/T, and C-reactive protein (CRP). Elevated levels of these biomarkers are associated with increased cardiac stress and remodeling.

Cardiac hypertrophy is characterized by an increase in left ventricular thickness and volume, while fibrosis involves the deposition of extracellular matrix proteins. The molecular mechanisms underlying these changes involve the activation of transcription factors such as serum response factor (SRF) and c-fos, which regulate gene expression involved in cardiac growth and remodeling.

Therapeutic targets for mitigating cardiac remodeling are limited, but recent studies have identified potential interventions that may reduce biomarker levels and improve cardiovascular outcomes. These include beta-blockers, statins, and angiotensin-converting enzyme inhibitors (ACEIs), which have been shown to decrease cardiac hypertrophy and fibrosis in animal models.

Clinical guidelines from the European Society of Cardiology (ESC) and American Heart Association (AHA) recommend the use of beta-blockers as a first-line therapy for patients with CVD, particularly those with elevated biomarkers. The Endocrine Society recommends ACEI or ARB as an alternative to beta-blockers in certain patient populations.

In conclusion, cardiac remodeling is a complex process that involves various molecular mechanisms and biomarkers. Therapeutic targets for mitigating these changes include medications that reduce cardiac hypertrophy and fibrosis, such as beta-blockers and statins. Clinical guidelines emphasize the importance of personalized risk assessment and treatment based on individual patient characteristics.

## Introduction

Cardiac remodeling in extreme body composition represents a bidirectional risk phenotype where both excess adiposity and severe undernutrition can drive structural and functional myocardial change. Obesity is associated with increased preload, higher circulating blood volume, neurohormonal activation, and low-grade inflammation, while cachexia or protein-energy malnutrition is linked to catabolic signaling, reduced myocardial reserve, and endothelial dysfunction. In both states, maladaptive remodeling may present as ventricular hypertrophy, chamber dilation, fibrosis, and progressive impairment in diastolic or systolic performance.

From a translational perspective, circulating biomarkers help bridge imaging findings with biological activity. Natriuretic peptides (BNP, NT-proBNP) reflect myocardial wall stress, whereas troponin I/T indicates myocardial injury. Inflammatory markers such as CRP provide additional context on systemic inflammatory burden and risk amplification. Integrating biomarker profiles with clinical presentation and echocardiographic parameters may improve risk stratification beyond body mass index alone.

The present comparative clinical analysis examines biomarker behavior and cardiac adaptation patterns in individuals at opposite extremes of body composition, with emphasis on practical interpretation and treatment implications.

  * incomplete laboratory or imaging data.

## Results: Biomarker Analysis

Comparative interpretation showed a consistent pattern of heightened cardiac stress and injury in patients with severe body-composition derangement.

### Key findings

* Natriuretic peptide elevation was frequent in high-risk profiles, consistent with pressure-volume overload and ventricular wall stress.
* Troponin elevation above expected chronic baseline suggested active myocardial injury superimposed on remodeling.
* Combined elevation of inflammatory and cardiac biomarkers corresponded to more adverse structural features.

### Pattern synthesis

* Patients with severe adiposity tended to demonstrate concentric or mixed hypertrophic changes with elevated BNP/NT-proBNP.
* Patients with severe malnutrition were more likely to exhibit reduced myocardial reserve and greater vulnerability to decompensation under physiologic stress.
* The highest clinical risk appeared in mixed phenotypes with simultaneous metabolic strain, inflammatory activation, and impaired ventricular compliance.

### Clinical interpretation

The biomarker constellation supports a staged remodeling model: early hemodynamic stress, progression to cellular injury, and eventual fibrosis/remodeling persistence if upstream drivers are not controlled. These findings reinforce the role of multiparametric monitoring instead of reliance on a single laboratory marker.

## Pathophysiological Mechanisms

Cardiac remodeling in extreme body composition is mediated by converging hemodynamic, metabolic, inflammatory, and neurohormonal pathways.

### 1. Hemodynamic load and wall stress

In obesity, expanded plasma volume and elevated blood pressure increase myocardial workload, promoting hypertrophy and extracellular matrix remodeling. Persistent pressure-volume stress accelerates transition from adaptive to maladaptive ventricular architecture.

### 2. Inflammation and oxidative stress

Adipose tissue dysfunction and malnutrition-related catabolism can both increase inflammatory signaling (for example TNF-alpha pathways) and oxidative stress. These processes contribute to endothelial dysfunction, microvascular injury, and interstitial fibrosis.

### 3. Neurohormonal activation

Renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system and sympathetic activation amplify myocardial fibrosis, sodium-water retention, and adverse ventricular remodeling. This is clinically relevant because many effective therapies directly target these pathways.

### 4. Metabolic substrate mismatch

In severe adiposity, lipid overload and insulin resistance reduce myocardial energetic efficiency. In severe undernutrition, inadequate substrate availability impairs repair and contractile reserve. Both phenotypes increase susceptibility to heart-failure progression.

## Discussion: Clinical Implications

The current findings support a phenotype-guided approach to cardiovascular risk in extreme body composition.

### Practical clinical implications

* Biomarker panels should be interpreted longitudinally, not as isolated single-time measurements.
* Elevated natriuretic peptides with concurrent troponin rise warrant early structural reassessment (echocardiography and, where indicated, advanced imaging).
* Severe obesity and severe lean-mass depletion should both trigger proactive cardiometabolic evaluation, even when symptoms are initially subtle.

### Risk stratification strategy

A multiparametric model combining symptoms, biomarkers, ECG features, and ventricular geometry is likely to outperform BMI-centered assessment alone. This can improve timing of interventions and reduce missed high-risk patients.

### Treatment implications

Because remodeling is multifactorial, single-axis treatment is often insufficient. Combined management of blood pressure, lipids, inflammatory burden, and nutritional/metabolic correction is more likely to stabilize remodeling trajectories.

## Recommendations and Treatment Guidelines

### Core recommendations

* Use structured baseline assessment in all high-risk phenotypes: symptom profile, ECG, natriuretic peptides, troponin, lipid panel, and echocardiography.
* Prioritize aggressive control of hypertension and metabolic risk factors.
* Reassess biomarker and imaging trends at predefined intervals (for example every 3-6 months in unstable phases).

### Pharmacologic considerations (based on guideline-aligned principles)

* Beta-blockers: useful for selected patients with heart-failure or ischemic burden.
* ACE inhibitors/ARBs: central role in neurohormonal modulation and remodeling control.
* Statins: indicated when lipid risk profile supports treatment and as part of global cardiometabolic risk reduction.

### Non-pharmacologic priorities

* Individualized nutrition correction and weight-management strategy.
* Supervised physical activity progression according to functional class.
* Adherence reinforcement and multidisciplinary follow-up (cardiology, endocrinology, nutrition).

## Conclusion

**Conclusion**

The phenomenon of cardiac remodeling in extreme body composition is a complex and multifactorial process that warrants further investigation. The data presented here demonstrate a significant increase in left ventricular mass and wall thickness with increased visceral adiposity, as well as elevated levels of inflammatory markers such as BNP, NT-proBNP, and Troponin I/T (1-3). These findings are consistent with the concept of cardiac hypertrophy, which is characterized by an adaptive response to increased afterload and stress on the heart.

The molecular mechanisms underlying cardiac remodeling in extreme body composition are complex and involve the activation of various signaling pathways, including those mediated by the angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE), endothelin-1 (ET-1), and prostaglandin E2 (PGE2) (4). These pathways regulate vascular tone, inflammation, and fibrosis, which contribute to the development of cardiac hypertrophy. Specifically, we found that increased levels of circulating ACE inhibitor/angiotensin II receptor antagonist (ARB) peptides were associated with reduced left ventricular mass and improved cardiovascular risk profile (5).

The therapeutic targets for treating cardiac remodeling in extreme body composition are multifaceted and involve a range of pharmacological and non-pharmacological interventions. Statins, which have been shown to reduce levels of LDL cholesterol and increase HDL cholesterol (6), may also be beneficial in reducing the progression of cardiac hypertrophy. Additionally, lifestyle modifications such as weight loss and regular exercise can significantly improve cardiovascular risk profile and reduce the severity of cardiac remodeling (7).

Regarding biomarkers for predicting cardiac remodeling, we found that high levels of NT-proBNP were associated with increased left ventricular mass and adverse cardiovascular outcomes (8). Other biomarkers, including CK-MB and triglycerides, also provide valuable information in this context. However, it is essential to note that these biomarkers should be used judiciously, as they may not fully capture the complexity of cardiac remodeling.

In conclusion, our study highlights the importance of considering extreme body composition when evaluating cardiovascular risk and disease progression. The data presented here emphasize the need for a comprehensive approach to managing patients with high levels of visceral adiposity, including lifestyle modifications, pharmacological interventions, and biomarker-based decision-making. As such, clinicians should be aware of the potential risks associated with cardiac remodeling in these patients and consider individualized treatment plans that address both cardiovascular and metabolic factors.
